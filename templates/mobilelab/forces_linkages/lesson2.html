Lesson 2 — Net Force & Mechanical Advantage

Background Reading

In every machine, parts push and pull on each other. The net force is the total of all these pushes and pulls, including their directions. If all forces balance, the part doesn’t speed up or slow down — it moves steadily or stays still.

Machines often use levers or linkages to give us mechanical advantage (MA). This tells how much the machine multiplies your input force:

MA = Output Force ÷ Input Force

When the input bar is long, you get more motion but less force. When the input is short and the output long, you get more force but less motion. This trade-off is why heavy-duty tools (like pliers or car jacks) move slowly but feel strong.

Vocabulary: net force – total push or pull; mechanical advantage – how much a machine multiplies your force; input – what you apply; output – what the machine delivers.
